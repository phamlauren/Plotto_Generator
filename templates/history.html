A novel plot suggestion tool created by William Wallace Cook for creative fiction writers for decades following its publication in 1928 - fondly considered "a textbook to which the lovable Mr. Cook devoted much care in his ardent desire to help others" by pulp editor and publisher H. Brainerd Hersey in 1937.3

"Masterplots with Interchangeable Clauses"4
The Plotto Masterplot generator implemented here is a simplified version which follows only the instruction Clause A + Clause B + Clause C, where there are 15 possible Clause As, 62 possible Clause Bs, and 15 possible Clause Cs. Plotto's 300 pages contain more indices (for example, of character specifications) and more complex instructions for categorizing and compiling conflicts, which are not implemented here.

William Wallace Cook (1867-1933)
“The Conflicts all come from the vast storehouse of Human Nature. They are there, millions upon millions of them, waiting for the imagination to select them and group them in an original combination.”
The Plotto's forefather: Ars brevis (1308)

In his own Art, Cook inherits a Llullian language, as do many of the writers on formal literary methods in the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries. Although the earliest mathematical and logical thought experiments related to combinatorics can be traced to long before Llull, Llull is still ahead of the formalization of combinatorics theory in the Renaissance. While Plotto lacks the diagrams of spinning wheels and there is no record that Cook envisioned a machine any more complex than one's own ability to choose random numbers which could be looked up in an index, Cook's language of combination and transcendentals like Human Nature certainly recall Llull's own spiritual fervor to achieve his Ars brevis and other works.

Cook glances back in history past over six hundred years to land on Llull’s Ars Brevis (1308), past the perennially resurfacing conflict: intelligible versus unintelligible reality - what can be taught and apprehended versus what cannot be - knowledge versus imagination - technique versus talent - discipline versus creativity. Interestingly, Cook himself does not oppose combinatorics and imagination - instead, he rearranges the equation altogether, to suggest that intellect is equal to combination plus imagination, intellect = combination + imagination: “The Conflicts all come from the vast storehouse of Human Nature. They are there, millions upon millions of them, waiting for the imagination to select them and group them in an original combination.”
